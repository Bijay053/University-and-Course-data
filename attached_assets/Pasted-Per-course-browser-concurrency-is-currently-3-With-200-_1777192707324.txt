Per-course browser concurrency is currently 3. With 200-course unis like CSU, this means a full scrape takes 60+ minutes wall-clock even when each page renders in 5-10 seconds.

Log evidence from CSU scrape (commit `edc05e20`):

```
[FALLBACK] [per-course browser ✓] https://study.csu.edu.au/courses/bachelor-education-primary  # rendered=1546527B (succeeded)
[FALLBACK] [per-course browser ✓] https://study.csu.edu.au/courses/bachelor-accounting       # rendered=1509101B (succeeded)
...
[TIMING] Concurrency: HTTP=4 Browser=3
```

Browsers succeed quickly. The bottleneck is the concurrency cap, not the per-page time.

## Fix

Raise per-course browser concurrency from 3 to 10 (or whatever the prod box can handle without OOM).

## Risk: RAM

Playwright Chromium ~ 200MB per worker. 10 concurrent = ~2GB. The DigitalOcean droplet has plenty of headroom (current usage 13%, ~30GB RAM total), but please confirm before committing:

```bash
free -h
# Aim for browser_concurrency * 250MB < (available_mem * 0.6)
```

If the box is constrained, 6 or 8 is fine. The exact number matters less than getting it off 3.

## Validation

After the change, fire CSU scrape:

**Good:**
```
[TIMING] Total: 5m 30s | Courses: 200 | Avg: 1.6s/course | Concurrency: HTTP=4 Browser=10
```

**Bad:**
```
# OOM in journalctl, Playwright crashes, droplet swap thrashing
```

If bad, drop concurrency to 6 and re-test.

## Why not other approaches

- **Skip per-course extraction for CSU** — too uni-specific, becomes tech debt. The data-quality risk of skipping is higher than the speed gain.
- **Disable Gemini after N zero-field returns** — too clever, hard to tune, hides genuine failures.
- **Lower per-course timeout** — already balanced, breaking sites we just fixed.

Concurrency is the lever. It's one constant in one file.

## Constraints

- Single small PR, just the constant
- Don't bundle with anything else (CSU fee parser, Gemini telemetry, etc.)
- Don't change other extractor concurrency — just the per-course browser pool
